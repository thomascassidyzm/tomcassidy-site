*Why this site talks back — and what it's quietly arguing for.*

A note before you read anything else here. Most of what you'll find on this site — the essays, the frameworks, the books — is published in a format I think matters as much as the ideas inside it. So it's worth a minute on what it is, especially since the best way to understand it is to use it: there's a guide on this page called Alexander, and you can ask him to explain himself. He will, in his own way. Go on — it's rather the point.

## The problem with the fixed page

The academic paper, and the book, are fixed artefacts. They're written once, at one altitude, for one imagined reader — and then every *actual* reader, whatever they already know and whatever they actually need, has to meet the text where it is. Pitched too high, you bounce off it; too low, you're bored; and either way, when you finish, you're on your own to work out what it means *for you*.

I've spent thirty years convinced that this is the real bottleneck. **Information was never the hard part. Implementation is.** People rarely fail to change because they lacked the facts — they fail because nothing helped them carry the facts across into their own particular life. A brilliant book compressed into a one-size-fits-all programme — the good idea, thinly disguised as a marketing funnel — is that bottleneck made visible.

## A living epistemic work

So this is an attempt at something else: a work that isn't fixed, but meets you where you are. A few things make it what it is, and they're deliberate.

- **It adapts to you.** The same piece can be taken as a quick overview, a full essay, or a formal treatment — and the guide pitches its answers to wherever you actually are, not to an average reader who doesn't exist.
- **Every claim wears its status.** Nothing here pretends to be more settled than it is. Claims are marked — *established, derived, contested, open* — so you can see at a glance what's load-bearing and what's still a live question. I'd rather show you the edges of my thinking than hide them.
- **The objections live inside, not outside.** The strongest arguments *against* an idea sit alongside it, as first-class citizens — not buried, not ignored. A view you can't see attacked is a view you can't really evaluate.
- **The guide draws out; it doesn't sell.** Alexander is Socratic by design. He builds from the page in front of you rather than reciting it, he won't collapse an open question into false certainty, and — this matters — he isn't trying to convince you of anything. If you see clearly and still disagree, that's a success. The seeing is the point, not the agreeing.

None of those pieces is, on its own, new. Explorable writing, living documents, status-tagging, AI tutors — they all exist. What I'm proposing is the *combination*, offered as an alternative to the journal and the static book: an artefact with epistemic honesty built into its bones, and a companion that helps you apply it to your case. Some novelty, a lot of reframing — most of it, I hope, helpful.

## Alexander

The guide is called Alexander, and the name is a small joke with a serious point. Socrates taught Plato, who taught Aristotle, who taught Alexander — so Alexander is the one who *inherits* the whole chain of thought and has to do something with it out in the world. That's the job: not to be the oracle, but to be the student who helps you think.

He's the same Alexander everywhere I use him — he isn't reinvented for each site — and on any given page he's reading *that* page and building from it. Which is why the most honest way to learn what he is, is to ask him. Open the guide and say: *explain yourself.* You'll get the method by watching it work on itself.

## The quiet argument

If this works — if a book or a theory can become something you *interrogate and apply* rather than something you *finish and shelve* — then it might be useful well beyond my own writing. An author who genuinely wants to be understood, and wants their ideas to land in each reader's specific situation, could publish this way instead of shipping a fixed text and hoping.

I'm not pitching that yet. The right order is to be the example first: put my own body of work into the format, and let it earn the idea. This site is that attempt.

It's also, deliberately, unfinished — an invitation more than a monument. The work I most want to spend the rest of my life on is an infinite game: the point isn't to win it or finish it, but to keep it going and bring others in. If any of this is useful, take it — and do your own thinking.
